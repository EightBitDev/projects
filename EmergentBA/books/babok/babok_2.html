Business Analysis Key Concepts

BACCM : Business Analysis Core Concept Model - is a conceptual framework for business analysis. It encompasses what business analysis is and what it means to those performing business analysis tasks regardless of perspective, industry, methodology, or level in the organization.

The BACCM can be used to:

  • Describe the profession and domain of business analysis
  • Communicate about business analysis with a common terminology
  • Evaluate the relationships of key concepts in business analysis
  • Perform better business analysis by holistically evaluating the relationships amoun the six concepts
  • Evaluate the impact of these concepts and relationships at any point during a work effort in order to establish both a foundation and path forward

The Six Core Conepts in the BACCM

  • Change - The act of transformation in response to a need.
  • Need - A problem or opportunity to be addressed.
  • Solution - A specific way of satisfying one or more needs in a context.
  • Stakeholder - A group or individual with a relationship to the change, the need, or the solution.
  • Value - The worth, importance, or usefulness of something to a stakeholder within a context.
  • Context - The circumstances that influence, are influenced by, and provide understanding of the change.

Key Terms

Design - is a usable representation of a solution. Design focuses on understanding how value might be realized by a solution if it is built.

Enterprise - is a system of one or more organizations and the solutions they use to pursue a shared set of common goals.

Organization - an autonomous group of people under the management of a single individual or board, that works towards common goals and objectives.

Plan - is a proposal for doing or achieving something. Plans describe a set of events, the dependencies among the events, the expected sequence, the schedule, the results or outcomes, the materials and resources needed, and the stakeholders involved.

Requirement - is a usable representation of a need. Requirements focus on understanding what kind of value could be delivered if a requirement is fulfilled.

Risk - the effect of uncertainty on the value of a change, a solution, or the enterprise.


Requirements Classification Schema

  • Business Requirements - Goals, objectives, and desired outcomes that describe why a change has been initiated.
  • Stakeholder Requirements - What stakeholder needs must be met in order to acieve the business requirements?
  • Solution Requirements - Description of the capabilities and qualities of a solution according to the stakeholder's requirements. Must be at a level of detail that allows the development team to create and impliment the solution. Solution requirements can be divided into two sub-categories.
    • Functional Requirements - Capabilities the solution must have, including behaviors, aesthetics, and data integration.
    • Non-Functional Requirements or Quality of Service Requirements - Includes less tangible items the solution must possess or qualities it must have (security layers, bandwidth consumption, performance benchmarks).
  • Transition Requirements - Items of a temporary nature. Capabilities the solution must have while in development but that are not needed once the solution is complete.

Requirements and Designs

At some level the business analyst is responsible for the design requirements of the solution. The same techniques used to define requirements are used to define the solution design.

REQUIREMENTS ARE FOCUSED ON NEED ---- DESIGNS ARE FOCUSED ON THE SOLUTION
